## Post 2: CBAM Definitive Phase (Article #2)

CBAM is no longer a future risk. It's a present obligation.

January 1, 2026: The EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism entered its definitive phase.

What changed:

→ Financial liability is now binding on imports
→ 50-tonne de minimis threshold (replaces €150 exemption)
→ Authorized CBAM Declarant status required by March 31, 2026
→ Certificates go on sale February 2027
→ First surrender deadline: September 30, 2027

Covered goods: cement, iron/steel, aluminium, fertilisers, electricity, hydrogen.

The cost formula is simple:
Embedded emissions × EU carbon price = your CBAM bill

At €70/tonne CO₂e, importing 1,000 tonnes of steel (1.8t CO₂e/tonne) = €126,000.

But here's what most companies miss:

Actual supplier data = lower costs.
Default values = penalizing and expensive.

The difference can be 20–40% of your total CBAM cost.

Supplier engagement isn't just compliance. It's cost reduction.

## Post 3: SBTi Rules Update (Article #3)

SBTi just made 2030 targets significantly easier.

April 2026 update:
→ Scope 1+2: 42% → 21% by 2030
→ Scope 3: 20% → 15% by 2030
→ Scope 1 and 2 now separated (no more hiding behind renewable energy)

Why SBTi did it:
Companies starting in 2025 had only 5 years to hit 42%. Many dropped out or never engaged. SBTi chose breadth over purity.

Why critics are worried:
1. IPCC still says 1.5°C needs 43% cut by 2030
2. Cumulative emissions — delayed early cuts consume carbon budget
3. Two-tier system — early adopters locked into 42%, new entrants get 21%

The hidden big change:
Scope 1 and 2 are now separated. Industrial companies can no longer use renewable electricity purchases to mask slow Scope 1 decarbonisation.

What should companies do?

New submissions: Model both 21% and 42% pathways. Consider stakeholder expectations.

Existing targets: Stay the course. The reputational risk of weakening likely outweighs any relief.
