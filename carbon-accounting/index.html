What Is Carbon Accounting?

Carbon accounting is the process of measuring, quantifying, and tracking greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions produced by an organisation, product, or activity. It provides the data foundation for climate strategy, regulatory compliance, and emissions reduction targets.

Most organisations follow the GHG Protocol, the world's most widely used standard for greenhouse gas accounting. Developed by the World Resources Institute and the World Business Council for Sustainable Development, it ensures consistency and comparability across reports.

Carbon accounting is no longer voluntary for many organisations. The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) requires approximately 50,000 EU companies to disclose Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions. Similar requirements are emerging in the UK, US, and other jurisdictions.

Why Carbon Accounting Matters

📋

Regulatory Compliance

CSRD, SECR, and emerging disclosure rules require verified emissions data. Non-compliance risks fines, reputational damage, and exclusion from public procurement.

💰

Cost Reduction

Identifying emission hotspots reveals energy inefficiencies. Organisations typically find 10–30% cost savings through fuel switching, process optimisation, and waste reduction.

🤝

Supply Chain Pressure

Large customers increasingly require supplier emissions data for their own Scope 3 reporting. Carbon accounting capability becomes a competitive requirement.

📈

Investor Expectations

Institutional investors use emissions data to assess climate risk. CDP, TCFD, and ISSB standards all require disclosure of GHG emissions and reduction targets.

The Three Scopes of Emissions

🔥

Scope 1: Direct Emissions

Emissions from sources your organisation owns or controls. Includes stationary combustion (boilers, furnaces), mobile combustion (company vehicles), and fugitive emissions (refrigerant leaks).

Scope 1 is typically the easiest to measure because you control the data sources. Fuel receipts, vehicle mileage, and maintenance records provide the activity data needed.

⚡

Scope 2: Energy Indirect

Emissions from the generation of purchased electricity, heat, steam, or cooling consumed by your organisation. These are indirect emissions, but the energy is consumed within your operational boundary.

The GHG Protocol requires two reporting methods: location-based (grid average) and market-based (contract-specific). Renewable energy contracts can significantly reduce market-based Scope 2.

🌐

Scope 3: Value Chain

All other indirect emissions occurring across your value chain. Covers 15 categories including purchased goods, capital goods, transport, waste, business travel, employee commuting, and product use.

Scope 3 typically represents 70–90% of total emissions. It is the most complex scope to measure but also offers the greatest reduction potential through supplier engagement and procurement decisions.

Regulatory Landscape

Carbon accounting requirements are expanding rapidly across jurisdictions. Understanding which regulations apply to your organisation determines what you must measure, how you must report, and when deadlines fall.

GHG Protocol

The foundation of corporate carbon accounting. The Corporate Standard defines Scope 1 and 2 boundaries. The Scope 3 Standard covers value chain emissions. The Product Standard enables product-level footprinting. Compliance with GHG Protocol is required by CDP, SBTi, and most regulatory frameworks.

CSRD — Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive

EU directive requiring large companies and listed SMEs to report detailed sustainability information under European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS). ESRS E1 (Climate Change) mandates Scope 1, 2, and 3 disclosure, climate risk assessment, and transition planning. Applies to approximately 50,000 companies.

CSRD Omnibus Guide →

ISSB — International Sustainability Standards Board

Developing global baseline sustainability disclosure standards. IFRS S2 covers climate-specific disclosures including GHG emissions across all scopes. Adopted by the UK, Australia, Singapore, Nigeria, and others. Creates convergence with CSRD and TCFD requirements.

SECR — Streamlined Energy and Carbon Reporting

UK legislation requiring quoted companies, large unquoted companies, and large LLPs to report energy use, GHG emissions, and intensity metrics. Simpler than CSRD but still mandates Scope 1 and 2 reporting and energy efficiency action.
