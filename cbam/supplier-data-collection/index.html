CBAM Supplier Data Collection Guide

How to collect, verify, and submit supplier emissions data to reduce your CBAM costs by 20-40%. A practical playbook for EU importers.

By Terrnix Intelligence • 12 min read

Executive Summary

Using actual supplier emissions data instead of CBAM default values can reduce your carbon border tax by 20-40%. But collecting this data requires a structured approach: identifying relevant suppliers, requesting specific data points, verifying accuracy, and submitting in the correct format.

This guide provides a step-by-step playbook for EU importers. It includes email templates, data request checklists, verification requirements, and common pitfalls to avoid.

Bottom line: Companies that invest in supplier data collection now will save hundreds of thousands in CBAM certificate costs. Those that rely on default values will overpay.

Why Supplier Data Matters

CBAM gives importers two options for calculating embedded emissions:

  • Default values: Conservative estimates published by the European Commission. Intentionally penalising to incentivise actual data collection.
  • Actual emissions data: Verified emissions data from your specific suppliers. Requires third-party verification but yields significantly lower costs.

Typical savings by material type:

  • Steel: 25-35% reduction with actual data
  • Aluminium: 20-30% reduction
  • Cement: 15-25% reduction
  • Fertilisers: 30-40% reduction

For a company importing 2,000 tonnes of steel annually, the difference between default and actual values can be €100,000+ per year.

Step 1: Identify Relevant Suppliers

Not all suppliers need to provide emissions data. Focus on:

  • Suppliers of CBAM-covered goods (cement, iron & steel, aluminium, fertilisers, electricity, hydrogen)
  • Suppliers representing >80% of your import volume (Pareto principle)
  • Suppliers with the highest emission factors (quick wins)

Action: Export your customs/import data for 2025-2026 and filter by CBAM CN codes. Rank suppliers by volume and identify your top 10-20 suppliers.

Step 2: Request Emissions Data

Send a structured data request to each supplier. Include:

  • Explanation of CBAM and why you need the data
  • Specific data points required (see checklist below)
  • Deadline for response
  • Offer of support or third-party verification assistance

Required Data Points

  • Direct emissions (Scope 1): Fuel combustion, process emissions from production
  • Indirect emissions (Scope 2): Electricity, heat, steam used in production
  • Production volume: Annual output in tonnes or relevant units
  • Emission factor: Tonnes CO2e per tonne of product
  • Calculation methodology: GHG Protocol, ISO 14064, or other recognised standard
  • Verification status: Third-party verified or self-reported

Email Template

Subject: CBAM Compliance — Emissions Data Request for [Product Name]

Dear [Supplier Name],

As you may know, the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) entered its definitive phase on January 1, 2026. As an EU importer of your [product], we are required to report the embedded emissions of our imports and purchase CBAM certificates.

To minimise our CBAM costs — and maintain our competitive purchasing position — we need verified emissions data for your production of [specific product].

Please provide the following by [date]:

  • • Direct (Scope 1) and indirect (Scope 2) emissions for [product] production
  • • Annual production volume
  • • Emissions calculation methodology used
  • • Third-party verification status (if available)

If you need assistance with emissions calculation or verification, we can recommend qualified providers or share our internal guidance.

Thank you for your partnership.
[Your Name]

Step 3: Verify Supplier Data

Third-party verification is mandatory when using actual emissions data for CBAM. Acceptable verification:

  • ISO 14064-3 verification statement
  • EU ETS verifier accreditation
  • Recognised national accreditation body

Red flags to watch for:

  • Inconsistent emission factors across similar products
  • Missing Scope 2 electricity emissions
  • Unrealistically low emission factors (possible data gaps)
  • No documentation of calculation methodology

Step 4: Calculate Embedded Emissions

For each imported product:

Embedded emissions = (Supplier emission factor) × (Import volume)

Example:

  • Supplier emission factor: 1.4 tCO2e/tonne steel
  • Annual import volume: 1,500 tonnes
  • Embedded emissions: 2,100 tCO2e
  • CBAM cost at €75/tonne: €157,500

Compare this to default values (typically 2.0-2.5 tCO2e/tonne for steel) to quantify your savings.

Step 5: Submit CBAM Reports

Quarterly CBAM reports must include:

  • Import volumes by CN code and supplier
  • Embedded emissions (actual or default)
  • Verification documentation for actual data
  • Carbon price paid in country of origin (if any)

Reports are submitted through the EU CBAM Transitional Registry.

Common Pitfalls

  • Waiting too long: Supplier data collection takes 2-3 months. Start immediately.
  • Requesting too much: Focus on top suppliers first. Don't try to collect data from every supplier simultaneously.
  • Ignoring verification: Unverified actual data is not accepted. Budget €2,000-5,000 per supplier for verification.
  • Poor documentation: Maintain records of all data requests, responses, and calculations for audit trails.
  • Not updating annually: Supplier emissions change. Refresh data annually for ongoing accuracy.
